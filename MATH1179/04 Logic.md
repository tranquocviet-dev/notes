# Logical reasoning within the confines of math papers
As with many things in life, there is such a thing as truth and fallacy in a vast subject such as math, though one might not realize it at first. Mostly in the form of ratings that people have given to the questions and answers in a sheet of paper, that is where most of everyone's perceptions about the true and false ends.

But beneath that there exist a sub-genre of math known as Logical math: using Math to solve logical questions, determine the fallacy of logic statements, and understand the importance of logic in the context of math and vice versa. It is only when we look further into the deep ends of math that we find one of the most fascinating studies in the field.

# So, what logic do we abide by when judging?
To understand this question, we have to first understand the most important kind of statement, being Proposition: a statement that has a value of true or false. These can be simplified into variables for more convenient reading, such as: I drink water - d; I am hapy - h. Moreover, propositions can be chained together to create a more complex statement: If I drink water, I am happy -
$$d arrow h$$
This create a very simple way to look at statements, simply using letters to indicate statement parts and judge the entire statement by using logic.

# So, we have figured out how to write them. What are the logical questions like?
Logical questions and statements from these symbols can be constructed with more notations to describe Logical questions, for example:
$$ d and  h $$
This is a **conjucntion** symbol, meaning that it only gets true when both variables are true. Which leads to a resulting logic table similar to this one:

| d   | h   | $d and h$ |
| --- | --- | --------- |
| T   | T   | T         |
| T   | F   | F         |
| F   | T   | F         |
| F   | F   | F         |
Another kind is the **OR** symbol: $$ d or h $$
This means that the statement is true at all times except when both variables are false:


| d   | d   | $d or h$ |
| --- | --- | -------- |
| T   | T   | T        |
| T   | F   | T        |
| F   | T   | T        |
| F   | F   | F        |
Another kind, the **XOR**, or exclusive OR, only accepts when either one is True.

There is also the case of **negation**, where the True and False state of a variable is swapped.

| d   | $minus d$ |
| --- | --------- |
| T   | F         |
| F   | T         |
