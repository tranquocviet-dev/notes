# What are relationships?
Relationships are mappings of sets, illustrating their connection using arrows to demonstrate, well, relationships. In fact, any arrow at all can be considered a relationship. Moreover, the arrows here hold immense power, capable of linking to something as broad as a set or a single element, and one would only have to consider where the arrowhead is placed to know what it links to. To keep it simple, one would just point at a emlement or the boundary of the set to switch between the two.
This makes relationships... simple, surprisingly. Why couldn't all relationships in life be this way?
# The biggest "set" of seperate given sets: the Cartesian Product
The Cartesian Product are all the possible pairings of two or more sets together, ordered. "Ordered" is really the keyword for the Cartesian Product. For instance, this one word made (a, b) different than (b, a)! This type was usually written like this $$ a times b $$

The awe of looking at stuff magically multiplying in numbers with a single word can only last so long, however. Two pairs of elements would turn from one set to two, but can you imagine how many would they multiply to if they had three, four, or more elements?

Actually, don't remind me about it. It still haunts me to this day.
